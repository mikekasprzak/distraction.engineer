Arrogantly I don't think I'm arrogant, but everyone is. Arrogance is a tool, the practice of hiding or burying anxieties for confidence. Socially you must exude confidence to be trusted, and you need trust for everything. Easier said than done when you overthink everything.

Two years ago when I rebooted my reboot-blog's reboot's reboot (yup), I chose to title it `The.Distraction.Engineer`. It's still one of my favorite identities I've crafted, but some recent experiences left me feeling I hadn't earned the name. I'm a relic with many successes and failures, but as I age the failures that have negatively affected me are piling up. Impostor syndrome is back baby!

In short I'm in my 40's, and financially I'm struggling more than ever. I started a new company in 2020, but I hate paperwork, and I hate selling. To me, selling is professional begging. My company actually did okay when my reputation was strong enough to find clients for me, but the idea of cold e-mailing clients repulses me. I know I shouldn't be bothered by it, as large companies have budgets to spend on marketing, but my same insecurities rear their ugly head here too: I don't feel like I've earned it.

With my finances in dire straits, I begrudgingly accepted I needed a supplemental income.


## Wants and needs

As a young adult, I needed to make video games, and I did that. From age 19 I worked my ass off in 3 different companies, back to back to back, building more than a dozen video games based on existing franchises. Almost nobody gets in to video games to make Barbie games though. I'd burnt-out of building licensed games. I needed to work on an original IP, my own IP.

`Sykhronics Entertainment` officially began in 2006. I had deep desire to understand advanced mathematics well enough to write 3D graphics and physics code from scratch, which I did. I successfully developed 2 IPs in my decade long self-employed stint (`PuffBOMB` and `Smiles`), but I was only able to commercially launch one of them (`Smiles`). While I worked on dozens other concepts and prototypes over the years, I fell into trap: the new-technology trap. You could say I have an addiction to learning. I'd get a core technology working, be that something like 2D skeletal animation or 3D voxels, and immediately I'd be hungry for the next one. While I was often ahead of the curve, I didn't have the patience to sit still long enough to productize my tech into a game.

My efforts became moot after the rise of middleware. By the early 2010's Unity had became a household name in the developer community, and I refused to touch it. I'd studied so much, finally free of aggressive deadlines of my professional gigs, but after `Smiles` I squandered my advantage by not committing to a concept or a tech I'd built. I couldn't compete with middleware users. What they got in two clicks would take me days or weeks. To concede and use middleware myself was inconceivable. I was arrogant, unwilling to admit defeat. I didn't want to let myself down. I didn't want to be wrong. I was stupid.
