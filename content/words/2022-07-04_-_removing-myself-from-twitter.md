For professional reasons I can't quit Twitter, but it's unhealthy for me to keep using it.

Out of frustration I recently switched my personal Twitter account to private. In the short term that means I've opted out of the algorithmic public discourse, though nearly 12k followers still have access to my words.

Since making the the switch, I've caught myself numerous times scrolling, getting triggered and nearly sucked into multiple conversations I couldn't meaningfully impact. It's not that nobody would see me, but before going private, only a subset of a subset of the 12k that follow me and who I'm replying to would see it. Things I share might do well, but I have little credibility to add outside my niche&mdash;These days that's "Game Jams", despite 20+ years programming, creating, and shipping games.

But now that I've gone private, it's nigh impossible for my words go viral. It's freeing. The fruitless situation I'm in makes now has made it easier to stop, before I've sunken an afternoon into debates I'm unqualified to participate in.

It can be difficult to resist hitting the Twitter echo chamber for gratification. My recently adopted pseudonym&mdash;the distraction engineer&mdash;isn't just a cheeky play on games and entertainment as distractions, but also a subtle reflection of my own struggle. It has taken me 4 decades to begin accepting my ADHD as something I don't need to be embarrassed about, but I'm actually proud to be in the business of distraction. Not all distractions are bad, but this one is for me.

After 15 years on the platform, I am no longer convinced "Twitter in moderation" is something I can realistically do. Twitter is a trap for the ADHD brain. For my mental health, I will be cutting back my socializing on the platform.

I'm not done with micro-blogging, but I'd rather my Twitter to be a "feed of me" instead of "my feed".
