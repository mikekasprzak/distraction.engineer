Some weeks ago, [Moxie Marlinspike](https://signal.org)&mdash;CEO behind the Signal messaging app&mdash;[announced](https://twitter.com/moxie/status/1480619206240522240) he was stepping down. This seemed abrupt, happening shortly after Twitter CEO Jack Dorsey announced his own departure. I had no idea who Moxie was, but I knew Signal. I was puzzled.

Yesterday his departure became [official](https://twitter.com/moxie/status/1489762162851516420). I was still puzzled.

Is Signal done? No, definitely not. What about this feature or&mdash;I'm asking the wrong question. Is Signal Messenger LLC done? Is it sustainable, and is Moxie no longer necessary? Unless I missed news of their offices catching fire, it must be sustainable, and he can step down.

I get it, but I'm not used to it. 


## You had one job
Every job I've had, every project I've been a part of, they've always demanded more. It's not that I don't like responsibility, but when you _can_ handle it, you're given more of it. I have mixed feelings about crunch. I was good at it, climbed the ladder quickly thanks to it, but it hurts every time. And today, over 20 years later, it's so much harder to do.

In 2018 I tried to buck the trend. I accepted job at a friend's game studio, but I didn't step into a management role. This time I would just be a senior developer, and that's it. That was fine for a while. We did some good work together, but after our gamble didn't pan out, there wasn't much left for me. No fault of anyone there, but I know myself: I'm not happy unless I'm moving forward. As expected, the company needed more from me, but what it needed was the wrong direction for me. I was going to miss the comradery, but what's right for the company wasn't right for me. I left at the end of 2019.

Fine. No matter what path I choose, it will always demand more of me. In that case, we need to get better at regulating the demands.


## Share the burden
I'm not a great delegator. That's part of how I ended up solely responsible for Ludum Dare. When everyone leaves a project, it either dies off, or _a Mike_ inherits everyone's responsibilities. If you're lucky and project continues to succeed, even more responsibilities emerge. That's the situation I found myself in.

It should be no surprise: yes, I hit my limit many years ago. But I grew up in crunch, so pushing myself&mdash;health or finances be damned&mdash;is just the norm for me. Reaching your limit doesn't have to mean you stop or quit, but it does means some of your responsibilities suffer. In Ludum Dare's case, it could keep going so long as I continued running events. But everything else, from software development to server maintenance, from support to compliance, from marketing to working with press, only so much of these ever got done.

Delegation isn't just handing out work. You can't delegate without people and resources to do it. I was lucky to get volunteers from time to time, but you can only ask so much before you're taking advantage of their kindness. Critical systems, deadlines, no one should have to do this sort of work without appropriate compensation. Even still, overwhelmed with my mountain of responsibility, I couldn't be there enough to make being a volunteer a positive experience. I was destined to leave a bad impression, whether I liked it or not.

Everyday the mountain grows, and it will continue growing until either I break, or I find a way out.


## Good company
Busy at my friend's game studio, I had almost no time to work on Ludum Dare. I'd book time off from work just to run events, and to keep up appearances at trade shows (GDC). At work we built an online multiplayer game, and I was the principal backend architect. Unfortunately the game never reached the level of success we hoped for, but in case it did, between all of my responsibilities, that left little time for me to rest or relax.

In 2019 I changed Ludum Dare's schedule from 3 to 2 events per year. I had to. I wasn't getting _the mountain_ under control while we ran 3 events every year.

When I left the game studio, all I knew is I needed something that paid better, and something that was less demanding. I still have projects I want to pursue outside Ludum Dare, but I also have Ludum Dare. I hadn't found a way to make this work yet. To keep the roof over my head, and to advance my interests.

My job search came to an end as covid became a reality in early 2020.
